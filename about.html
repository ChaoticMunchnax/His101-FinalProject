Meet Augustus Caesar:

Augustus Caesar was born as Gaius Octavius Thurinus in Rome on September 23, 63 BC, to a family of modest means but respectable ancestry (Polybius 6.11.11-6.18.3). Augustus was the grand-nephew of Julius Caesar, who adopted him as his son and heir.

He was only eighteen when Caesar was assassinated in 44 BC, leaving him as the main beneficiary in his will (Suetonius).

Early Life:

Augustus Caesar had an excellent education, which was provided by his mother, Atia, and his great-uncle, Julius Caesar. He studied rhetoric, law, and Greek literature under the best teachers in Rome (Polybius 6.11.11-6.18.3). During his adolescence, Augustus was a member of a special unit of the Roman army, where he learned military tactics and leadership skills (Suetonius).

Political Career:

Augustus Caesar began his political career as a member of the Second Triumvirate, a political alliance formed in 43 BC with Mark Antony and Marcus Lepidus (Livy). The alliance aimed to avenge Julius Caesar's death and establish their power in Rome. Augustus proved to be a skilled politician and military commander, leading the alliance to victory in several battles, including the Battle of Philippi in 42 BC (Polybius 6.11.11-6.18.3). After the alliance dissolved, Augustus became the first Emperor of Rome and ruled for over forty years (McDermott). He implemented many reforms that stabilized Rome's economy and society, including the construction of new public works and the creation of a professional army (Salmon). Augustus also initiated a series of moral and social reforms aimed at restoring traditional Roman values (Starr, "Horace and Augustus").

Augustus' Personal and Professional Qualities:

Augustus was widely respected for his intelligence, charisma, and leadership abilities (McDermott). He was a skilled orator and writer, and his ability to communicate effectively with the people was a key factor in his political success (Starr). Augustus was also known for his strategic vision and his ability to anticipate and respond to potential threats to the stability of the Roman state (Ramage).

Augustus’ Legacy

Augustus' legacy in Roman history is significant. He is credited with bringing stability and prosperity to the Roman state after decades of political turmoil and civil war (Livy). His reign marked the beginning of the Pax Romana, a period of relative peace and prosperity that lasted for over 200 years (Starr). Augustus' impact on Roman culture and society was profound, and his legacy can still be felt today in the form of the many monuments and buildings he commissioned during his reign (Augustus).
